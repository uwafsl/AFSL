UW researchers beg FAA to regulate drones, allow testing

Dec 17, 2014, 1:32pm PST Updated: Dec 17, 2014, 4:47pm PST

Seattle researchers are growing weary as the Federal Aviation Administration prepares to miss another self-made deadline for its regulation of small unmanned aerial systems or vehicles (UAS/UAV), set for the end of this year.

The lack of guidance is hurting research abilities at the University of Washington and other public research institutes, researchers there say.

The Congressional deadline for the FAA's drone regulation is Sept. 30, 2015. But the agency has said for months that it will push out regulations for small UAS – those under 55 pounds, and generally the most sought-after regulations – earlier than that.

Allen Kenitzer, spokesman for the FAA, said the agency is planning to have rules for small UAS by the end of the year.

In October, research scientist Dr. Christopher Lum, said he was told to expect the ruling any day. But it still hasn't come. That's making it difficult for universities such as UW to reach their innovation potential.

In UAS research the possibilities are limitless, said Al Creigh, one of Lum's undergraduate students.

Lum's team of about 25 graduate and undergraduate students at the University of Washington Department of Aeronautics and Astronautics is working to develop a UAV that can determine location without GPS.

It is one of a handful of UAV research groups at UW.

Another group, led by associate professor Kristi Morgansen, is trying to make more agile UAVs and have recently been working on developing underwater UAVs.

While the FAA has yet to complete its regulations for use of UAV/UAS, the regulatory agency did established six flight test centers throughout the country where the machines can be tested.

Washington state doesn't have a test center – the nearest ones are Alaska (which includes sites in Hawaii and Oregon), Nevada and North Dakota. So if UW researchers want to test their creations, they have to get approval through a special FAA exemption known as a certificate of waiver acceptance.

The University of Washington has three active waivers. The application process takes significant time and requires filling out a lot of paperwork. Without clear guidelines on what is and isn't allowed, researchers are left guessing and hoping they don't break the rules.

Morgansen described it as a catch-22.

"You need to collect data to show the system is safe," she said. "But there is not an easy way to do that because the FAA wants to know the system is safe before you fly it around."

Before the popularity of UAVs exploded, hobbyists who flew model airplanes or helicopters had unclassified, but well understood, rules they abided by: Not too high, not near anything flying, not near many people.

The FAA has since released hobbyist guidelines, but those don't account for the research and commercial facilities that want to do drone testing and research.

UW Dean of Engineering Michael Bragg said the FAA generally develops new policies in five to 10 years, but now the regulatory agency is moving much faster in response to the popularity of drones.

But until those regulations are out, research work is stunted because without regulations researchers are worried they could push boundaries or break rules without knowing it and risk losing their research abilities.

"The last thing we want is for a university to ruffle feathers," Lum said.

Creigh said that research in the United States is years behind other countries that have UAV regulations.

"I wish there was more direction on where we could go, and what we could do," Lum said.

Lum respects what the FAA is trying to do, and acknowledges the difficult task at hand to balance research and public concern. But that doesn't make it any easier on his research group.
